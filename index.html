1.2 Business Entities
Sole Trader

One person owns and runs the business. Unlimited liability.

Advantages
  • Full control over decisions
  • Keep all profits
  • Simple to set up, low cost
  • Flexibility, quick decisions
Disadvantages
  • Unlimited liability (personal assets at risk)
  • Limited capital, hard to grow
  • No cover when owner ill/holiday
  • Skills limited to one person
Applied Example

A local shawarma stall in Dubai, owner takes all profit but also bears all debt. If sued, personal savings and home are at risk.

Partnership

2 to 20 partners share ownership. Usually unlimited liability unless LLP.

Advantages
  • Shared capital and workload
  • Complementary skills
  • Shared risk
  • Simple formation
Disadvantages
  • Unlimited liability
  • Disagreements between partners
  • Profits split
  • Slow decision-making
Applied Example

Deloitte, PwC, McKinsey are all partnerships, partners share ownership, profits, and legally-unlimited liability in many jurisdictions.

Private Limited Company (Ltd / LLC)

Shares sold privately. Shareholders have limited liability. Separate legal identity.

Advantages
  • Limited liability, shareholders only lose their investment
  • Separate legal entity
  • Can raise capital via private share sale
  • More credibility than sole trader
Disadvantages
  • Cannot sell shares publicly
  • More admin/legal requirements
  • Financial accounts must be filed
  • Complex to set up vs sole trader
Applied Example

On Holding AG was a Swiss LLC before its 2021 NYSE IPO. A Dubai café LLC, owners' personal homes are protected if the business fails.

Public Limited Company (PLC)

Shares sold to the public on a stock exchange. Full limited liability.

Advantages
  • Massive capital raising via IPO
  • Limited liability for shareholders
  • Enhanced public profile/credibility
  • Easier for employees to own shares
Disadvantages
  • Loss of control, shareholders vote
  • Short-term shareholder pressure on profits
  • Expensive listing & compliance costs
  • Hostile takeover risk
Applied Example

On Holding AG (ONON) raised ~$750m in its 2021 IPO. Emirates NBD (ENBD) is listed on the Dubai Financial Market, open to any investor.

Cooperative

Owned and run by its members for mutual benefit. Democratic (1 member = 1 vote).

Advantages
  • Democratic, members have equal say
  • Profits shared among members
  • Ethical business model
  • Strong community loyalty
Disadvantages
  • Slow decision-making
  • Difficult to raise large capital
  • Members may lack business expertise
  • Conflicts among members
Applied Example

REI (US outdoor cooperative), members pay $30 lifetime fee, get dividends. Mondragon (Spain), world's largest cooperative with 81,000 worker-owners.

NGO / Non-profit

Exists for social/charitable purposes. Surplus reinvested into mission, not distributed.

Advantages
  • Tax exemptions
  • Strong public trust & brand
  • Grant funding available
  • Motivates values-driven staff
Disadvantages
  • Cannot distribute profit
  • Dependent on donations/grants
  • Governance complexity
  • Hard to attract high-salary talent
Applied Example

Médecins Sans Frontières (MSF), operates in 70+ countries, 100% funded by donations, no shareholders. One Billion Lives Foundation, NGO redistributing technology access.

Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)

A business's commitment to ethical behaviour, environmental care, and community contribution beyond legal requirements. Can be philanthropic (charitable), strategic (integrated into business model), or greenwashing (superficial).

Applied, Genuine CSR

Patagonia donates 1% of sales to environmental causes, uses recycled materials, and in 2022 transferred ownership to a trust, profit permanently funds climate work. Their "Don't Buy This Jacket" campaign built authentic loyalty.

Applied, Greenwashing

Volkswagen Dieselgate (2015), marketed diesel cars as "clean" while software cheated emissions tests. Fined $14.7bn, brand damaged severely. Contrast with genuine CSR to explain the difference.

Diseconomies of Scale

Beyond optimal scale, average costs can rise as firm grows too large.

Causes
  • Poor communication across large hierarchies
  • Low morale, workers feel lost, alienated
  • Coordination problems between departments
  • Bureaucracy slows decision-making
  • Principal-agent problem (managers act in self-interest)
Applied Example

General Electric (GE) diversified so far it could no longer manage its divisions efficiently, spun off into 3 separate companies by 2024.

1.6 Multinational Corporations (MNCs)

An MNC operates in multiple countries, typically with headquarters in one nation and subsidiaries/manufacturing in others.

Host Country Benefits
  • FDI (Foreign Direct Investment), capital inflows
  • Employment creation
  • Technology transfer and skills development
  • Tax revenue for government
  • Infrastructure investment
Host Country Concerns
  • Profit repatriation, money leaves host country
  • Cultural erosion, local businesses displaced
  • Environmental exploitation
  • Tax avoidance via transfer pricing
  • Low wages, poor working conditions
Applied, Tesla Gigafactory Shanghai

Tesla built Giga Shanghai in 2019, China's largest FDI project at the time. Benefits: 9,000+ local jobs, supply chain development, technology transfer. Concerns: profits repatriated to US, Chinese competitors (BYD, NIO) gained indirect knowledge spillover, environmental impact of battery production.

2.3 Motivation Theories
Taylor's Scientific Management (1911)

Workers are rational economic beings motivated primarily by money. Break tasks into smallest components, find the "one best way" to do each, pay by results (piece-rate).

Strengths
  • Increased productivity in manufacturing
  • Clear targets for workers
  • Still used: Amazon warehouse piece-rate scanning
Criticisms
  • Dehumanising, treats workers as machines
  • Ignores social and psychological needs
  • Monotonous → low morale, high turnover
Applied, Amazon Fulfillment Centers

Workers scan a target of 300+ items/hour, tracked by productivity software. Piece-rate style incentives. High productivity BUT staff turnover ~150% annually, injuries 2× industry average. Taylor's theory works for output, it fails for wellbeing.

Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (1943)

Five tiers of human needs, satisfied from bottom upward. A lower need must be mostly met before higher needs motivate.

Implications for managers
  • Identify which level employees are currently at
  • Match rewards to unmet needs
  • Physical/safety first, then social/esteem
Criticisms
  • Rigid hierarchy, people need multiple levels simultaneously
  • Cultural differences, collectivist societies value social > esteem
  • Hard to measure which level someone is at
Applied, Ritz-Carlton Hotels

Ritz-Carlton addresses all five layers: Physiological, above-average pay; Safety, permanent contracts, clear HR policies; Social, "Ladies and Gentlemen serving Ladies and Gentlemen" team ethos; Esteem, Employee of the Month, published guest compliments; Self-actualisation, each employee given $2,000/day discretionary budget to solve any guest problem without manager approval. Result: industry-leading retention and Net Promoter Scores.

Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory (1959)

Distinguishes between factors that cause dissatisfaction (hygiene factors) and factors that cause true motivation (motivators). Removing hygiene factors only prevents unhappiness, it doesn't motivate.

Hygiene Factors (Dissatisfiers)

Must be present to avoid dissatisfaction, but don't directly motivate:

  • Salary and pay
  • Job security
  • Working conditions / environment
  • Company policy and administration
  • Relationships with supervisors
Motivators (Satisfiers)

These genuinely increase motivation and job satisfaction:

  • Achievement and recognition
  • Responsibility
  • Advancement / promotion
  • Personal growth
  • The work itself being interesting
Applied, John Lewis Partnership (UK)

John Lewis is a worker-owned partnership where all "Partners" (staff) share annual profits. Hygiene factors: good pay, secure jobs, quality canteen. Motivators: ownership stake creates genuine responsibility and achievement. Result: consistently higher customer satisfaction and lower staff turnover than competitors like M&S.

2.4 HR Metrics
Labour Turnover= Staff leaving per yearAverage staff employed × 100%
High turnover = costly recruitment + lost knowledge. Target: <15% in most industries
Labour Productivity= Output per periodNumber of employees
Output in units, revenue, or tasks. Key efficiency metric.
Absenteeism= Days absent per yearTotal possible working days × 100%
UK national average ~2.6%. High absenteeism signals poor morale.
Applied, Pebble Smartwatch (startup)

Pebble raised $10.2m on Kickstarter (2012) but had extremely high labour turnover (~60% in year 2). Reason: founder-only equity, no formal HR structure, unclear roles. Herzberg's hygiene factors unmet. By 2016, Pebble was acquired by Fitbit for ~$40m, a fraction of its potential, partly due to talent retention failure. Use this to discuss how HR strategy directly impacts business performance.

3.5 Depreciation

The systematic allocation of a non-current asset's cost over its useful life. Two IB methods:

Straight-Line Method
Annual Depreciation= Cost − Residual ValueUseful Life (years)

Example: Machine costs $50,000, residual $5,000, life 5 years.
Annual dep. = (50,000−5,000)/5 = $9,000/year

Reducing Balance Method
Annual Depreciation=Rate% × Net Book Value

Example: NBV $50,000, rate 20%.
Year 1: $10,000. Year 2: 20% × $40,000 = $8,000. Year 3: 20% × $32,000 = $6,400.
Higher depreciation early, matches asset use for vehicles/tech.

Key Definitions
Favourable Variance
Actual result is better than budgeted, revenue higher OR costs lower than planned.
Adverse Variance
Actual result is worse than budgeted, revenue lower OR costs higher than planned.
Zero-based budgeting
Every budget starts from zero each period, every cost must be justified. Prevents budget padding.
Incremental budgeting
Previous year's budget + adjustment. Simple but can perpetuate inefficiencies.

Coca-Cola extends its mature product through: new flavours (Cherry, Vanilla, Zero Sugar), new packaging (mini cans, glass bottles for premium), new markets (Africa expansion), and seasonal campaigns. The core product is decades old but extension strategies keep it in maturity, not decline.

Applied, LEGO

LEGO nearly went bankrupt in 2003. Extension via licensing (Star Wars, Harry Potter, Marvel sets), LEGO movies, video games, and adult-targeted sets (LEGO Architecture, Botanicals) repositioned a "declining" toy into sustained growth, revenue tripled by 2015.

5.5 Break-Even Analysis

The break-even point (BEP) is the output level where total revenue = total costs, i.e. profit is zero. Below BEP the firm makes a loss, above it makes a profit.

Worked Example
VariableValue
Selling price per unit$10
Variable cost per unit$6
Fixed costs$20,000
Contribution per unit$10 − $6 = $4
Break-even point20,000 ÷ 4 = 5,000 units
Break-even (units)= Fixed CostsContribution per unit
20,000 ÷ 4 = 5,000 units. At this output: Revenue = 5,000 × $10 = $50,000 = Total cost (20,000 + 5,000×6 = $50,000) ✓
Related Formulas
Contribution per unit=Price − Variable cost/unit
Total Contribution=Contribution/unit × Units sold
Profit=Total Contribution − Fixed Costs
Margin of Safety=Actual output − BEP output
If selling 7,000 units: MOS = 7,000 − 5,000 = 2,000 units (the cushion before making a loss)
Target Profit Output= Fixed Costs + Target ProfitContribution per unit
For $12,000 profit: (20,000+12,000)/4 = 8,000 units
Break-Even Chart (BEP correctly at 5,000 units / $50,000)

The break-even point is where the Revenue line crosses the Total Cost line, at exactly 5,000 units and $50,000. Left of this = loss; right of this = profit. The vertical gap between revenue and total cost beyond BEP = profit at that output.

Effects of Changes on Break-Even
ChangeEffect on BEPWhy
↑ Selling priceBEP falls (fewer units needed)Higher contribution per unit
↑ Variable costBEP rises (more units needed)Lower contribution per unit
↑ Fixed costBEP risesMore contribution needed to cover FC
↓ Selling priceBEP risesLower contribution per unit
Benefits of Break-Even Analysis
  • Simple visual tool for decision-making
  • Shows margin of safety
  • Helps set output and pricing targets
  • Useful for "what-if" scenario planning
  • Supports loan applications (shows viability)
Limitations
  • Assumes all output is sold (unrealistic)
  • Assumes costs/price are linear (ignores discounts, economies of scale)
  • Fixed costs may change at higher output (stepped costs)
  • Only suitable for single-product analysis
  • Static, ignores market changes over time

BCG Matrix

Portfolio analysis mapping products by market share (high/low) vs market growth (high/low).

Applied, Apple's BCG Portfolio

Star: iPhone (high share in still-growing premium smartphone market). Cash Cow: MacBook/iPad (mature market, strong share, funds R&D). Question Mark: Vision Pro (new AR market, uncertain share). Dog: iPod (discontinued 2022, declining market, replaced by phones). A balanced portfolio uses Cash Cows to fund Stars and Question Marks.

Decision Trees HL

A diagram showing quantitative decision options, their probabilities, and expected values (EV). Squares = decisions, circles = chance nodes.

Expected Value (EV) = Σ (probability × payoff)
Then subtract the cost of each decision to get net EV. Choose highest net EV.
Limitations of Decision Trees
  • Probabilities are estimates, often inaccurate
  • Ignores qualitative factors (brand, ethics, staff morale)
  • Assumes a static environment
  • Bias in assigning probabilities by managers

Definition Sheet
Key terms for 2-mark "define" questions · grouped by unit · use precise IB wording
Unit 1 · Business Organization
Business
An organisation that combines human, physical and financial resources to produce goods or services to satisfy customer needs.
Entrepreneur
An individual who plans, organises and takes the financial risk of running a business in pursuit of profit.
Limited liability
Shareholders are only liable for the debts of the business up to the amount they invested; personal assets are protected.
Unlimited liability
Owners are personally responsible for all business debts, risking personal assets.
Stakeholder
Any individual or group with an interest in, or affected by, the activities and decisions of a business.
CSR
Corporate Social Responsibility, a business's obligation to act ethically and consider its social and environmental impact beyond legal requirements.
Economies of scale
Cost advantages a business gains as output increases, reducing average cost per unit.
Multinational corporation (MNC)
A business that operates production or service facilities in more than one country.
Merger
When two businesses agree to combine and form one new legal entity.
Acquisition
When one business buys a controlling interest (over 50%) in another business.
Unit 2 · Human Resources
Leadership
The ability to influence, inspire and direct people towards achieving organisational goals.
Motivation
The internal and external factors that drive an individual to take action and put effort into their work.
Labour turnover
The percentage of the workforce that leaves a business over a given period, usually one year.
Appraisal
The formal assessment of an employee's performance against set objectives.
Job enrichment
Giving employees more challenging and meaningful tasks with greater responsibility (vertical loading).
Job enlargement
Increasing the number of tasks an employee performs at the same level (horizontal loading).
Empowerment
Giving employees the authority and autonomy to make decisions about their own work.
Span of control
The number of subordinates directly reporting to a manager.
Delayering
Removing one or more levels of hierarchy from an organisational structure.
Unit 3 · Finance & Accounts
Gross profit
The profit made from trading, calculated as sales revenue minus cost of goods sold.
Net profit
The profit remaining after all expenses, interest and tax have been deducted from gross profit.
Liquidity
The ability of a business to pay its short-term debts as they fall due.
Working capital
The funds available for the day-to-day running of a business; current assets minus current liabilities.
Depreciation
The reduction in the value of a non-current asset over its useful life.
Insolvency
When a business cannot pay its debts as they fall due (a cash flow problem).
Bankruptcy
A legal status when a business or individual cannot repay debts and assets are liquidated.
Investment appraisal
The quantitative techniques used to assess the financial viability of a capital investment decision.
Budget
A quantitative financial plan for a future period, setting targets for revenue and expenditure.
Variance
The difference between a budgeted figure and the actual figure achieved.
Unit 4 · Marketing
Marketing
The management process of identifying, anticipating and satisfying customer needs profitably.
Market share
The proportion of total sales in a market held by one business or product, expressed as a percentage.
Market orientation
A business approach that focuses on identifying and meeting customer needs through market research.
Product orientation
A business approach focused on the product itself and its quality, assuming demand will follow.
USP
Unique Selling Point, a feature that distinguishes a product from competitors.
Product life cycle
The stages a product passes through from launch to withdrawal: introduction, growth, maturity, decline.
Penetration pricing
Setting a low initial price to quickly gain market share.
Price skimming
Setting a high initial price on a new product, lowering it over time.
Marketing mix
The combination of elements (7Ps) used to market a product effectively.
Unit 5 · Operations
Break-even point
The level of output where total revenue equals total costs, so neither profit nor loss is made.
Contribution
The amount each unit sold contributes to covering fixed costs, after variable costs are paid.
Margin of safety
The amount by which actual output exceeds the break-even output.
Fixed costs
Costs that do not change with the level of output (e.g. rent, salaries).
Variable costs
Costs that change directly with the level of output (e.g. raw materials).
Capacity utilisation
The proportion of maximum possible output that a business is currently producing.

Exam Technique, Mark Band Tips
  • "Evaluate/Discuss" (top band): always reach a justified conclusion, don't just list pros/cons. Use phrases like "On balance...", "The most significant factor is...", "This depends on..."
  • Application marks: name the specific business and use figures from the case study. Generic answers cap at low bands.
  • Analysis: build chains of reasoning, "Lower price → higher demand → more revenue → but lower margin → so net effect depends on PED."
  • Use the tools: if a question suits SWOT/Ansoff/BCG, structure your answer around it explicitly.
